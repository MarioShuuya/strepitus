Dynamometer Modes

A dynamometer (dyno) is a device that applies a controlled load to the engine, allowing you to measure and regulate its output. In this simulation the dyno sits on the crankshaft and can operate in three modes:

Speed
Holds a target RPM. A PI controller automatically adjusts the throttle so the engine converges on whatever RPM you set. The dyno applies a light absorber load (proportional to speed). This is the default mode — set an RPM and the engine will reach and maintain it. Use Sweep to automatically ramp through the full RPM range and plot a torque/power curve.
Load
Applies a constant braking torque. You set both the throttle and the load (in N·m). The engine will settle at whatever RPM balances its torque output against the fixed load. Open the throttle wider and RPM rises; increase the load and RPM drops. This simulates a simple absorption dynamometer like a water brake or eddy-current brake.
Power
Absorbs a constant power. The dyno computes the required braking torque each instant as τ = P / ω (a hyperbolic load curve). At low RPM the load torque is high; at high RPM it’s low — but absorbed power stays constant. You control the throttle manually. This mode answers the question: “at what RPM and throttle can this engine sustain a given power output?”
K — PI controller gain (Speed mode only). Higher values make the controller more aggressive.
